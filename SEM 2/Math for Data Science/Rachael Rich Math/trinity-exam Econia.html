(c) Given a random variable $X$ with PDF $ f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} $ for $ x \geq 0 $, derive the expressions for mean and variance of $X$.

For an exponential distribution with PDF $ f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} $:

  • Mean ( $ \mu $ ): $$ E(X) = \int_0^\infty x \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \frac{1}{\lambda} $$

Detailed derivation (maybe unnecessary)

Certainly! Let's delve deeper into the calculation of the expected value (mean) of an exponential random variable.

An exponential random variable $X$ with parameter $\lambda$ has the probability density function (PDF):

$$ f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0 $$

The expected value (mean) of $X$ is given by:

$$ E(X) = \int_0^\infty x \cdot f(x) \, dx $$

Substitute the PDF $f(x)$ into the integral:

$$ E(X) = \int_0^\infty x \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx $$

Using integration by parts to solve this integral. --Integration by parts is given by:

$$ \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du $$

Let:

$$ u = x \quad \Rightarrow \quad du = dx $$ $$ dv = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = -e^{-\lambda x} $$

Using these, the integration by parts formula becomes:

$$ \int x \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = x (-e^{-\lambda x}) \Big|_0^\infty - \int (-e^{-\lambda x}) \, dx $$

Evaluating the first term:

$$ x (-e^{-\lambda x}) \Big|_0^\infty = \left[ -x e^{-\lambda x} \right]_0^\infty $$

As $ x \to \infty $, $ x e^{-\lambda x} $ approaches 0 because the exponential term $ e^{-\lambda x} $ decays much faster than the linear term $ x $ grows. So,

$$ \left[ -x e^{-\lambda x} \right]_0^\infty = 0 - (0 \cdot e^{0}) = 0 - 0 = 0 $$

Evaluating the integral $ \int -e^{-\lambda x} \, dx $:

$$ \int -e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \frac{-e^{-\lambda x}}{-\lambda} = \frac{e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda} $$

Suubstituting this back into our integration by parts formula:

$$ \int_0^\infty x \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = 0 - \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda} \right]_0^\infty $$

Evaluate this term:

$$ \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda} \right]_0^\infty = \frac{e^{-\lambda \cdot \infty}}{\lambda} - \frac{e^{-\lambda \cdot 0}}{\lambda} = \frac{0}{\lambda} - \frac{1}{\lambda} = 0 - \frac{1}{\lambda} = -\frac{1}{\lambda} $$

Thus, we have:

$$ E(X) = \int_0^\infty x \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \frac{1}{\lambda} $$

Therefore, the expected value (mean) of an exponential random variable $X$ with parameter $\lambda$ is $ \frac{1}{\lambda} $.

  • Variance ( $ \sigma^2 $ ): $$ Var(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 $$ First, find $ E(X^2) $: $$ E(X^2) = \int_0^\infty x^2 \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} $$ Therefore, $$ Var(X) = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \left(\frac{1}{\lambda}\right)^2 = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} $$

(i) Matrix Inversion and Its Importance in Data Science

Matrix inversion is the process of finding a matrix $A^{-1}$ such that when it is multiplied by the original matrix $A$, it results in the identity matrix $I$. In mathematical terms, if $A$ is an $n \times n$ matrix, its inverse $A^{-1}$ satisfies the equation:

$$ A \cdot A^{-1} = A^{-1} \cdot A = I $$

where $I$ is the identity matrix of the same dimension.

Importance in Data Science:

  1. Solving Linear Systems: Many problems in data science, such as linear regression, require solving systems of linear equations. Matrix inversion provides a way to solve these equations efficiently.
  2. Optimization: Algorithms for optimization often require the inversion of matrices, especially in methods like the Newton-Raphson method.
  3. Transformation and Projections: In tasks like Principal Component Analysis (PCA), matrix inversion is used to project data into a lower-dimensional space.
  4. Stability and Condition Number: Inverting matrices can help in understanding the stability of a system, as the condition number of a matrix involves its inverse.

LU Decomposition of a Matrix $ A $

LU decomposition is a factorization of a matrix $ A $ into the product of a lower triangular matrix $ L $ and an upper triangular matrix $ U $. This decomposition can be written as:

$$ A = LU $$

where:

  • $ L $ is a lower triangular matrix (all elements above the diagonal are zero).
  • $ U $ is an upper triangular matrix (all elements below the diagonal are zero).

Significance of LU Decomposition in Data Science

  1. Solving Systems of Linear Equations: LU decomposition simplifies the process of solving linear equations. Given $ Ax = b $, we can write $ LUx = b $. By defining $ y = Ux $, we solve $ Ly = b $ and then $ Ux = y $. This is computationally efficient.

  2. Matrix Inversion: LU decomposition can be used to find the inverse of a matrix. Once $ A $ is decomposed into $ L $ and $ U $, the inverse can be found by inverting $ L $ and $ U $ and then multiplying them.

  3. Determinant Calculation: The determinant of $ A $ can be easily computed from $ L $ and $ U $. Since $ A = LU $, $ \text{det}(A) = \text{det}(L) \cdot \text{det}(U) $. For triangular matrices, the determinant is the product of the diagonal elements, making it straightforward.

  4. Numerical Stability: LU decomposition helps in improving the numerical stability of algorithms. Decomposing $ A $ into $ L $ and $ U $ reduces rounding errors in numerical computations.

  5. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors: LU decomposition is a step in algorithms for finding eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which are important in many data science applications such as PCA and spectral clustering.

  6. Optimization Problems: Many optimization algorithms, such as those used in machine learning and statistical inference, require the solution of linear systems or matrix inversions, where LU decomposition is a critical tool.

Let $X_1, ..., X_{10}$ be a random sample from a population with mean 50 and standard deviation 4. Let $\bar{X}$ be the sample mean. Find the expected value and standard deviation of $\bar{X}$.

Solution:

  • Sample size $ n = 10 $.
  • $ \bar{X} $ is the sample mean.

Expected Value of $ \bar{X} $:

The expected value of the sample mean $ \bar{X} $ is equal to the population mean $ \mu $. $$ E[\bar{X}] = \mu = 50 $$

Standard Deviation of $ \bar{X} $:

The standard deviation of the sample mean $ \bar{X} $ is given by: $$ \text{SD}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $$ where $ \sigma $ is the population standard deviation and $ n $ is the sample size.

Substitute $ \sigma = 4 $ and $ n = 10 $: $$ \text{SD}(\bar{X}) = \frac{4}{\sqrt{10}} $$

Calculating this gives: $$ \text{SD}(\bar{X}) = \frac{4}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 1.2649 $$

Therefore, the expected value of $ \bar{X} $ is $ 50 $ and the standard deviation of $ \bar{X} $ is approximately $ 1.2649 $.

Solution:

To find the probability that the sample mean among 1000 customers differs from the true mean by more than 5 minutes, we'll use the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). Here are the steps to solve this:

Given:

  • Population standard deviation ($ \sigma $) of monthly minutes = 120 minutes.
  • Sample size ($ n $) = 1000.

Step 1: Calculate the Standard Error of the Mean

The standard error of the mean ($ SE $) is given by: $$ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $$

Substitute $ \sigma = 120 $ minutes and $ n = 1000 $: $$ SE = \frac{120}{\sqrt{1000}} $$ $$ SE = \frac{120}{31.62} $$ $$ SE \approx 3.79 $$

Step 2: Determine the Probability using the Normal Distribution

The probability that the sample mean differs from the true mean by more than 5 minutes can be approximated using the normal distribution, due to the CLT.

Let $ \bar{X} $ be the sample mean. We are interested in finding $ P(|\bar{X} - \mu| > 5) $.

$$ P(|\bar{X} - \mu| > 5) = P\left(\left|\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{SE}\right| > \frac{5}{SE}\right) $$ $$ = P\left(\left|\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{3.79}\right| > \frac{5}{3.79}\right) $$ $$ \approx P\left(\left|\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{3.79}\right| > 1.32\right) $$

Step 3: Calculate the Z-score and Find the Probability

Question 5(d):

To determine the sample size $ n $ required for estimating a proportion with a desired margin of error and confidence level, we use the formula derived from the normal approximation to the binomial distribution.

Given:

  • Desired margin of error ($ E $) = 3 percentage points = 0.03 (since 3% = 0.03 in decimal).
  • Desired confidence level = $ 1 - \alpha $ = 95%, corresponding to a significance level $ \alpha = 0.05 $.
  • Estimated proportion ($ \hat{p} $) from pilot studies or initial estimates = 0.35 (35%).

Formula for Sample Size $ n $:

The formula for calculating the sample size $ n $ is: $$ n = \frac{Z^2 \cdot \hat{p} \cdot (1 - \hat{p})}{E^2} $$

Where:

  • $ Z $ is the Z-score corresponding to the desired confidence level $ 1 - \alpha $.
  • $ \hat{p} $ is the estimated proportion.
  • $ E $ is the margin of error.

Step-by-Step Calculation:

  1. Find the Z-score: For a 95% confidence level, $ Z $ is approximately $ 1.96 $ (using standard normal distribution tables).

  2. Substitute the values into the formula: $$ n = \frac{(1.96)^2 \cdot 0.35 \cdot (1 - 0.35)}{(0.03)^2} $$

  3. Calculate $ n $: $$ n = \frac{3.8416 \cdot 0.35 \cdot 0.65}{0.0009} $$ $$ n = \frac{0.873964}{0.0009} $$ $$ n \approx 971.07$$

  4. Round up to the nearest whole number: Since $ n $ must be a whole number, round up $ 971.07 $ to $ 971 $.

Conclusion:

To ensure that there is only a 5% chance that the estimate from the survey will differ from the true proportion by more than 3 percentage points, the sample size $ n $ must be at least $ 971 $.
